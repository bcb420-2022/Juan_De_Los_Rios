The dataset we are accessing is GSE166847
Platform title : Illumina HiSeq 2500 (Homo sapiens)
Submission date : Mar 14 2013
Last update date : Mar 27 2019
Organism : Homo sapiens (taxid: 9606).

The dataset contains 20 samples, 5 samples for each of the 4 experimental conditions. The experimental conditions are human primary astrocytes being exposed to 0, 1, 3, or 4 bouts of 0.1 mmol/l of low glucose for three hours per day for 4 days. Naturally, the HPA that was exposed to 0 bouts of low glucose act as the control group that other samples can utilize as a benchmark.
The purpose of the experiment is to measure the effect of recurrent hypoglycaemia on HPA cells. HPA cells are typically responsible for regulating blood sugar levels, but repeated instances of hypoglycaemia impair the cell’s ability to perform this function. Our dataset thus serves the purpose of allowing us to analyze gene expression relative to instances of hypoglycaemia in order to develop a model for the reaction to and longterm impact of recurrent hypoglycaemia in HPA cells.

Transfering dataset to data frame

The supplementary file associated with GSE166847 is difficult to work with in the format it comes in, so we transfer it to a data frame to ease future data manipulation. We also collect the different types of data categories now to be used during normalization later on.

counts_frame <- data.frame()
for(i in 1:dim(supp_counts_data)[1]){
  counts_frame <- rbind(counts_frame, 
                        unlist(strsplit(supp_counts_data[i,], ","))[c(1, 7:26)])
}
colnames(counts_frame) <- 
                  unlist(strsplit(colnames(supp_counts_data), ","))[c(1, 7:26)]
colnames(counts_frame)[1] <- "Gene"
counts_frame[, 2:21] <- lapply(counts_frame[, 2:21], as.numeric)
data_categories <- unlist(lapply(colnames(counts_frame)[2:21], 
                        FUN=function(x){unlist(strsplit(x, split = "_[0-9]"))}))

Cleaning and Filtering Data

Duplicate genes and gene expressions

Our dataset contains 56870 unique genes.
Since these values are the same, we have no need to worry about duplicate genes skewing our analysis.

Filtering statistically insigificant data

Although our dataset records the expression levels of several genes, a decent portion of these genes are not expressed enough to meaningfully contribute to our analysis. We’ll use the edgeR package to filter them out of our dataset.

cpms = edgeR::cpm(counts_frame[, 2:21])
keep = rowSums(cpms > 1) >= 5   #Our dataset uses 5 samples per group
filtered_counts_frame <- counts_frame[keep,]

The dataset originally contained 56870 genes. After being filtered, it only contains 13691 genes. Thus, we filtered out 43179 outliers.

Unique gene expressions

The final step in filtering our data is confirming that each gene we analyse is expressed uniquely. We can check if one of our antecedent (AH) samples have unique expression for each gene to accomplish this. The first antecedent sample contains 13691 genes and reports 13691 unique values for gene expression. Thus, we know gene expression is unique for each gene and requires no further attention.

Having finished all our data filtering, we can compute our final coverage. Of the 56870 genes we started with, we will only be analyzing 13691 of them. Thus, our final coverage is 0.240742.

Assigning proper gene symbols

Our dataset already assigned all its genes to a HUGO symbol so we simply confirm they were mapped appropriately. We then take these symbols and map them to their corresponding gene in our filtered data frame.

rownames(filtered_counts_frame) <- select(org.Hs.eg.db, 
                                           keys = filtered_counts_frame$Gene, 
                                           columns = 'SYMBOL', 
                                           keytype = 'SYMBOL')[[1]]

Notably a few of the genes in the dataset, such as RP5-857K21.8, have no known HUGO symbol. We allow them to retain their current name since we gain nothing by trying to map these genes to some other symbol like Ensemble IDs.

Normalization process

We utilize TMM normalization both because it is specialized for use with RNAseq data and because we cannot assume that any variation introduced into our data by technical means has a similar impact across all genes. Thus we cannot utilize normalization by distribution. We also cannot assume that gene expression in terms of mRNA quantity is equivalent across all samples so we cannot normalize by library size.

counts_matrix <- as.matrix(filtered_counts_frame[, 2:21])
rownames(counts_matrix) <- rownames(filtered_counts_frame)
d = edgeR::DGEList(counts = counts_matrix, group = data_categories)
d = edgeR::calcNormFactors(d)
